The universe is full of mysteries

SUN

The Sun is the star at the center of the Solar System. It is a nearly perfect ball of hot plasma, heated to incandescence by nuclear fusion reactions in its core, radiating the energy mainly as visible light, ultraviolet light, and infrared radiation. It is by far the most important source of energy for life on Earth. Its diameter is about 1.39 million kilometers (864,000 miles), or 109 times that of Earth. Its mass is about 330,000 times that of Earth, and it accounts for about 99.86% of the total mass of the Solar System. Roughly three quarters of the Sun's mass consists of hydrogen (~73%); the rest is mostly helium (~25%), with much smaller quantities of heavier elements, including oxygen, carbon, neon and iron.According to its spectral class, the Sun is a G-type main-sequence star (G2V). As such, it is informally, and not completely accurately, referred to as a yellow dwarf (its light is closer to white than yellow). It formed approximately 4.6 billion years ago from the gravitational collapse of matter within a region of a large molecular cloud. Most of this matter gathered in the center, whereas the rest flattened into an orbiting disk that became the Solar System. The central mass became so hot and dense that it eventually initiated nuclear fusion in its core. It is thought that almost all stars form by this process.

MARS

Mars, an obsolete name in the east, is Mars - the fourth closest planet to the Sun in the solar system and the second smallest after Mercury. Named after the god of war in Roman mythology, the widespread spread of iron oxide on the surface of this planet gives it a red appearance, and it is often described as a "red planet". Mars is a terrestrial planet (mostly composed of silicate rocks and metals) with a thin atmosphere and surface features reminiscent of craters formed after a lunar collision, as well as volcanoes, valleys, deserts, and polar ice caps. The period of rotation and the seasons of Mars are similar to those of Earth; the inclination of the axis forms the seasons. Mars is the second highest mountain in the solar system (the first highest on the planet) - Mount Olympus and one of the largest canyons, Valles Marineris. The smooth Borealis basin in the Northern Hemisphere covers 40% of the planet and may be a feature of a giant collision.

VENERA

The Venera (Russian: Вене́ра, pronounced [vʲɪˈnʲɛrə], which means "Venus" in Russian) program was the name given to a series of space probes developed by the Soviet Union between 1961 and 1984 to gather information about the planet Venus. Ten probes successfully landed on the surface of the planet, including the two Vega program and Venera-Halley probes, while thirteen probes successfully entered the Venusian atmosphere. Due to the extreme surface conditions on Venus, the probes could only survive for a short period on the surface, with times ranging from 23 minutes to two hours. Space probe Venera on Venus's surface (artist recreation). The Venera program established a number of precedents in space exploration, among them being the first human-made devices to enter the atmosphere of another planet (Venera 3 on 1 March 1966), the first to make a soft landing on another planet (Venera 7 on 15 December 1970), the first to return images from another planet's surface (Venera 9 on 8 June 1975), the first to record sounds on another planet (Venera 13 on 30 October 1981), and the first to perform high-resolution radar mapping scans (Venera 15 on 2 June 1983).

JUPITER

Jupiter is the fifth largest planet in the solar system and the first largest. Although Jupiter is one thousandth of the mass of the Sun, it is a gas giant with a mass 2.5 times the mass of the other planets. Jupiter has been known to astronomers since ancient times. Its name comes from Jupiter, the main deity in Roman mythology. Jupiter has enough light to create a shadow on Earth. It is the third natural celestial body in the night sky after the Moon and Venus. Although Jupiter is mainly composed of hydrogen, a quarter of its mass is Helium. Jupiter does not have a rocky surface like other gas giants, but it is likely to have a nucleus composed of solids. Due to its rapid rotation around its axis, the planet is below the poles and above the equator. Observations of the planet's outer atmosphere are made up of strips that can be observed, and hurricanes are observed at the intersections of these sections. An example of this is the Great Red Spot, which has been observed by telescopes since the 17th century. Jupiter has a weak ring and a strong magnetosphere surrounding it. Jupiter has 79 known satellites, four of which, called Galileo satellites, were discovered in 1610 by Galileo Galilei. Ganymede, the largest of Galileo's satellites, is larger than Mercury.

EARTH

Earth is the third planet from the Sun and the only astronomical object known to harbor life. While large amounts of water can be found throughout the Solar System, only Earth sustains liquid surface water. About 71% of Earth's surface is made up of the ocean, dwarfing Earth's polar ice, lakes and rivers. The remaining 29% of Earth's surface is land, consisting of continents and islands. Earth's surface layer is formed of several slowly moving tectonic plates, interacting to produce mountain ranges, volcanoes and earthquakes. Earth's liquid outer core generates the magnetic field that shapes Earth's magnetosphere, deflecting destructive solar winds.Earth's atmosphere consists mostly of nitrogen and oxygen. More solar energy is received by tropical regions than polar regions and is redistributed by atmospheric and ocean circulation. Water vapor is widely present in the atmosphere and forms clouds that cover most of the planet. Greenhouse gases in the atmosphere like carbon dioxide (CO2) trap a part of the energy from the Sun close to the surface. A region's climate is governed by latitude, but also by elevation and proximity to moderating oceans. Severe weather, such as tropical cyclones, thunderstorms, and heatwaves, occurs in most areas and greatly impacts life.

NEPTUNE

Neptune is the eighth and farthest known planet in the solar system. Neptune, which belongs to the group of giant planets, ranks fourth in the solar system in diameter and third in mass. The mass of Neptune is 17 times the mass of the Earth. This figure is 15 times that of Neptune's twin Uranus. Neptune is located at a distance of 30.1 AV from the Sun (4.50 × 109 km) and completes its motion around the Sun with 164.8. The name Neptune comes from Roman mythology. Neptune was considered a sea god in Roman mythology. The planet's astronomical symbol, ♆, is also associated with the three-toothed spear depicted in Neptune's hand. Research led by Alexei Buva has led to the idea that an unknown planet could cause unexpected changes in the orbit of Uranus. Neptune was later observed on September 23, 1846, very close to the position predicted by Urban Levery by Johann Gottfried Gal. Although Neptune's largest satellite, Triton, was discovered shortly after the planet's discovery, the remaining 14 satellites were discovered in the 20th century. Neptune looks small because it is so far from Earth, which makes it difficult to observe with telescopes. On August 25, 1989, the Voyager 2 spacecraft made observations near Neptune.

URANUS

Uranus is the seventh planet closest to the Sun. It is the third largest planet in the solar system by radius and the fourth largest by mass. The composition of uranium is the same as that of Neptune, both contain the same chemical elements, which distinguish them from the giant gas giants Jupiter and Saturn. For this reason, scientists classify Uranus and Neptune as "ice giants" to distinguish them from gas giants. Although the atmosphere of uranium is similar to that of Saturn and Jupiter, mainly in terms of hydrogen and helium content, it contains more ice along with ammonia, methane and other hydrocarbons. Uranium has the coldest atmosphere in the solar system with a minimum temperature of 49 K (−224 ° C; −371 ° F). Uranium has a complex layered cloud structure. The clouds in the lowest layer are thought to contain water, and the clouds in the upper layer are thought to contain methane. The interior of Uranus consists mainly of ice and rocks. The uranium system has a unique configuration that distinguishes it from other planets. Its axis of rotation is sloping, and therefore the region that is the equator of other planets is its north and south poles. Images from Voyager 2 in 1986 showed Uranus as an uncharacteristic planet with no cloud clusters or storms associated with other giant planets in the visible light. In Uranus, wind speeds reach 250 meters per second (900 km / h).

SATURN

Saturn, whose obsolete name is Saturn, is the second largest planet in the solar system after Jupiter and the sixth most distant from the Sun. Saturn's radius is a gas giant about nine times the radius of Earth. Although the density of Saturn is about one-eighth of the density of the Earth, its volume is 95 times the volume of the Earth. The name of the planet comes from Saturn, the god of Roman mythology, and the astronomical symbol (♄) and oxygen compounds). This nucleus is also thought to be surrounded by metallic hydrogen, with liquid hydrogen and liquid helium in the middle layers, and a gas layer outside the Frenkel line. Saturn's upper atmosphere is composed of ammonia crystals, so it looks light yellow. The electric current in the metallic hydrogen layer is thought to have created the planet's magnetic field. Although this current is weaker than that of the Earth, due to the large size of the planet, its magnetic moment is 580 times greater than the Earth's magnetic moment. The strength of Saturn's magnetic field is equal to one-twentieth of the strength of Jupiter's magnetic field. Saturn's upper atmosphere is mostly mild and calm. However, long-lasting atmospheric phenomena can also be observed. Saturn has a wind speed of 1,800 km / h, which is stronger than Jupiter and weaker than that of Neptune. In January 2019, researchers based their research on Saturn's C-ring and determined that a Saturn's day was 10 hours 33 minutes 38 seconds (± 1 minute 19 seconds).
